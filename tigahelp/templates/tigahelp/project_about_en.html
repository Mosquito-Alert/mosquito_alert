What is Mosquito Alert?

Mosquito Alert is a citizen science platform that facilitates collaboration between various actors to study, monitor and control mosquito species that carry diseases like dengue, Zika, chikungunya and West Nile virus.

The project gives key roles to citizens, scientists, public health experts and educators. Mosquito Alert lets anyone collect and share data (1) and engage in data validation alongside professional entomologists, who label mosquito reports and respond to participants (2). Once validated, data is made available to the general public through an interactive web map (3). It is also relied on by scientists (4) and by public health agents (5) to study and monitor disease vector mosquitoes.

Why is Mosquito Alert needed?

In recent decades the number of invasive mosquitoes and emerging mosquito-borne diseases has increased on a global scale. New invasive species, mainly of the genus Aedes , have settled in new areas and continue to expand. Aedes mosquitoes are important from a health perspective because of their ability to transmit diseases like dengue, Zika and chikungunya. Likewise, the common mosquito (Culex pipiens) is important because it can transmit the West Nile virus. The risk mosquitoes pose to public health makes it important to monitor their populations.

Given the complexity of the problem, Mosquito Alert opens up mosquito surveillance and research to the public, allowing collaboration between different actors through its app and its other platforms and communities.

What data is collected?

Data is collected on 5 different mosquito species: the tiger mosquito (Aedes albopictus), the yellow fever mosquito (Aedes aegypti), the Asian bush mosquito (Aedes japonicus), the Korean mosquito (Aedes koreicus), and the common mosquito (Culex pipiens).

For these species, data is collected on adult mosquitoes and their breeding sites and bites. All of this data is important for better understanding these species, and for monitoring and controlling them.

Mosquito Alert also collects the approximate locations of participants' devices, sampling these locations at 5 random times each day unless the participant has opted out of this. Locations are masked on a grid of 0.025 x 0.025 degrees latitude and longitude (approximately 2x2 km) before being sent to the Mosquito Alert server. For privacy reasons, only the grid square identifier is actually shared with Mosquito Alert, not the precise location. This information is highly valuable from a scientific perspective, as it enables researchers to make better estimates about mosquito populations, distinguishing, for example, places with few mosquitoes from places with few participants (but potentially many mosquitoes). Participant locations provide information about what is known in ecology as the sampling effort.

What is the data used for?

Here are some examples:

  • The reports people send to Mosquito Alert have proven in recent years to be an effective early warning system against the invasion of new mosquitoes. The system is used to monitor established mosquito populations and discover new populations.
  • Observations of mosquitoes, breeding sites and bites help scientists and public health managers to assess the risk and manage disease vector populations. In order to estimate the abundance of mosquitoes in a given site, it is important to adjust observations based on sampling effort. To do this, the Mosquito Alert app collects data on the approximate position of the device at random times of the day. Positions are masked on a grid of approximately 2x2 km to protect privacy.
  • Work is currently underway to develop a real-time, participatory and dynamic epidemiological risk prediction system.
